Task Understanding (LLMs)
Language → Intent → Action plan

What it is: The robot listens to a spoken or typed instruction — "pick up the red box and put it on the shelf" — and converts it into a plan it can act on. This is done by large language models (LLMs), the same underlying technology as ChatGPT.

Why it matters at Flexion: Flexion's platform is described as combining "natural language task comprehension" as the top layer, so the robot doesn't need to be manually reprogrammed for every new job. A warehouse worker can just tell it what to do.

What PhDs might ask about: Prompt engineering for robotic planning, chain-of-thought reasoning, grounding language in physical actions (called "language grounding").

// Term explanations — click to expand
Prompt engineering for robotic planning ▼
Designing the exact structure and wording of instructions you give an LLM so it reliably outputs valid, executable robot action sequences — not just natural language. E.g., "You are a robot planner. Output only a JSON array of actions from the allowed set." The challenge: LLMs were trained on human text, not robot APIs, so they need guidance to stay within what a robot can actually do.
Chain-of-thought reasoning ▼
Making an LLM "think step by step" before giving a final answer — rather than jumping straight to output. For robots: "First identify the target object → determine its 3D position → plan the arm trajectory → output the action." This dramatically improves reliability on multi-step tasks. PhDs debate when chain-of-thought is needed vs. overkill, and whether it generalizes beyond training distribution.
Language grounding ▼
Connecting abstract words to concrete, perceivable things the robot can act on. When a human says "pick up the red mug near the sink," every word needs to map to something in the robot's sensor data. Words like "near," "heavy," or "fragile" have no inherent meaning to a robot — grounding is the process of giving them one, usually by linking language to visual observations or physical properties. A core open problem in robotics AI.

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) Models
Seeing the world + turning it into movement

What it is: VLA models are AI models that take in visual input (what cameras see), language context (what the robot has been told), and output actions (move arm left, close gripper). It's the bridge between understanding and movement.

Ayça Takmaz's expertise lives here. Her PhD work (ETH Zurich / Google / NVIDIA) focused on 3D scene understanding — teaching computers to understand what objects are, where they are in 3D space, and what they can be used for (called "affordance understanding"). Her paper OpenMask3D allows robots to identify any object by description, not just a fixed list.

Synthetic training data: Flexion trains these models inside simulators, generating huge amounts of fake-but-realistic data, rather than relying on expensive real-world demonstrations. This is a key differentiator.

What PhDs might ask about: Affordance understanding, open-vocabulary detection, sim-to-real domain gap for visual inputs, data efficiency in VLA training.

// Term explanations — click to expand
Affordance understanding ▼
Recognizing not just what an object is, but what you can do with it. A door handle affords "grasping and pulling." A flat surface affords "placing things on it." Ayça Takmaz's SceneFun3D work enables robots to identify these functional properties from 3D scans — critical for manipulation in real environments where the robot can't have been pre-programmed for every possible object.
Open-vocabulary detection ▼
Identifying any object by free-text description rather than a fixed list of categories. Traditional object detectors recognize a set of ~80 known classes (COCO dataset). Open-vocabulary models like OpenMask3D can find "the yellow mug with a chipped handle" — something never seen in training data — by leveraging vision-language models. This is what makes robots generalizable to new environments.
Sim-to-real domain gap (visual) ▼
Training VLA models on synthetic rendered images and deploying on real camera feeds introduces a "domain gap" — differences in lighting, texture, noise, and color that can cause the model to fail. Bridged by photorealistic rendering (Flexion uses high-fidelity simulators), domain randomization (varying lighting/textures during training), and fine-tuning on small amounts of real data. Still an open research challenge.

Reinforcement Learning (RL)
Learning through trial and error — at massive scale

What it is: Instead of being programmed with rules ("if obstacle, turn left"), the robot is rewarded for good outcomes and penalized for bad ones — and it figures out the rules itself. Think of teaching a dog: you don't explain physics, you give treats.

Nikita Rudin's breakthrough paper "Learning to Walk in Minutes" (CoRL 2022, 1,150+ citations) showed that training thousands of robots simultaneously in simulation on a single GPU could compress months of training into minutes. This is the core of Flexion's speed advantage — they call it "massively parallel RL."

David Hoeller extended this: his ANYmal Parkour paper (Science Robotics 2024) showed RL-trained robots can do athletic, agile movements — jumping, climbing — behaviors once thought impossible to program manually.

What PhDs might ask about: PPO vs. SAC policy gradient methods, curriculum learning, reward shaping, massively parallel training infrastructure.

// Term explanations — click to expand
PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) ▼
The most common RL algorithm used for robot locomotion (including by Flexion's founders). PPO updates the policy in small, conservative steps — it clips updates so the new policy never strays too far from the old one, preventing catastrophic performance collapse. Think of it as cautious improvement: keep what works, refine gradually. The "proximal" in the name refers to staying close to the previous policy.
Curriculum learning ▼
Training on progressively harder tasks rather than throwing the robot into full difficulty from day one. Example sequence: flat ground → slight slopes → stairs → stairs with obstacles → stairs with obstacles and dynamic pushes. Without curriculum learning, the robot almost never discovers successful behaviors by accident when the task is hard. With it, each stage provides a useful starting point for the next.
Reward shaping ▼
Designing what the robot gets rewarded for during training — arguably the hardest part of applied RL. Too sparse (reward only for reaching the goal): the robot almost never succeeds early enough to learn. Too dense (reward every joint movement): the robot finds bizarre exploits that maximize points without actually solving the task. Good reward shaping guides learning without over-constraining the solution. PhDs spend careers on this.
Massively parallel training ▼
Running thousands of robot simulations simultaneously on a single GPU. Rudin's "Learning to Walk in Minutes" ran 4,096 virtual robots in parallel — collecting millions of timesteps of experience per second of real time. This is why training that once took days or weeks now takes minutes. NVIDIA Isaac Lab (co-created by Flexion's founders) is the tool that makes this possible. It's Flexion's core speed advantage.

Whole-Body Control (Transformer-Based)
Coordinating all limbs simultaneously

What it is: A humanoid robot has ~30+ joints. Whole-body control means coordinating all of them at once, not just the arm or just the legs. A transformer (the same architecture behind GPT) processes all joint states together and outputs a coherent motion plan.

The sim-to-real problem: Physics in simulation is never 100% identical to the real world. Flexion specializes in making models trained in simulation transfer reliably to real hardware — Nikita Rudin's research specifically addressed this by training across randomized simulation conditions so the robot becomes robust to real-world variation.

Julian Nubert bridges into this: his work on state estimation and LiDAR-based localization gives the robot an accurate, real-time sense of where it and objects around it are — essential for whole-body control to work safely.

What PhDs might ask about: Domain randomization, the sim-to-real gap, MPC vs. learned control trade-offs, state estimation and sensor fusion.

// Term explanations — click to expand
Domain randomization ▼
Intentionally varying simulation parameters during training — robot mass, friction coefficients, motor delay, ground texture, disturbance forces — so the robot learns a policy robust to that variation. The insight: if you train across enough randomized conditions, the real world is just one more "random domain." It's the main technique for bridging simulation to reality without needing a perfect simulator.
Sim-to-real gap ▼
The discrepancy between how physics works in simulation vs. the real world. Friction models are approximate. Motors have latency. Joints have backlash. When a policy trained purely in simulation is deployed on real hardware, these mismatches cause failure. Bridging strategies: domain randomization, system identification (measuring the real robot and updating the sim to match), and actuator modeling. Flexion's technical core competency.
MPC vs. learned control ▼
Model Predictive Control (MPC): uses an explicit physics model to predict future states and optimize actions — precise and interpretable, but computationally expensive and brittle when the model is wrong. Learned control (RL): learns a direct policy from experience — fast at runtime, handles model uncertainty implicitly, but requires massive training. Flexion bets on learned control at scale. ETH RSL historically blended both — this is the active debate in the field.
State estimation ▼
Fusing data from multiple sensors (IMU, joint encoders, cameras, LiDAR) to estimate the robot's current physical state — position, velocity, orientation, contact forces — accurately and in real time. The policy only works if it knows where the robot is and what it's doing. Julian Nubert's PhD focused on the hard cases: when can state estimation be trusted, and when is it likely wrong?

Perception & Mapping (LiDAR + Vision)
Sensing the physical world in 3D

What it is: Before the robot can do anything, it needs to know where it is and what's around it. LiDAR (laser-based distance sensors) creates a precise 3D point cloud of the environment. SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) uses that data to build a live map and track the robot's position within it — simultaneously.

Julian Nubert is Flexion's perception lead. His PhD research produced papers on self-supervised LiDAR odometry and localizability estimation — essentially: "when can the robot trust its own position estimate, and when should it be cautious?" This is critical safety-adjacent work for real-world deployment.

Ayça Takmaz adds the object layer: her SceneFun3D (CVPR 2024, Oral — a top honor) enables robots to understand not just that an object exists, but what actions it affords — is it a handle? A surface to place things on?

What PhDs might ask about: LiDAR odometry, point cloud processing, SLAM algorithms, 3D instance segmentation, localizability estimation.

// Term explanations — click to expand
LiDAR odometry ▼
Estimating how the robot has moved by comparing successive LiDAR scans. If scan A and scan B look slightly shifted, you can infer the robot moved by that amount. Works like GPS-free positioning using laser geometry — critical indoors and in GPS-denied environments. Error accumulates over time (called drift), so it's typically fused with other sensors via SLAM to correct it.
Point cloud processing ▼
A point cloud is a set of 3D coordinates where LiDAR beams hit surfaces. Processing involves: filtering noise, downsampling for speed, finding planes and edges, detecting objects, and computing surface normals. It's the raw input to almost every 3D perception pipeline. Unlike images, point clouds are unordered and sparse — standard CNNs don't apply directly, requiring specialized architectures (PointNet, VoxelNet, etc.).
SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) ▼
The chicken-and-egg problem: you need a map to localize, but you need localization to build a map. SLAM solves both at once by maintaining a probabilistic estimate of the robot's position while incrementally building the map from sensor readings. Classic approaches use graph optimization (iSAM, GTSAM). Modern approaches (like Nubert's) use deep learning for better robustness to dynamic environments and perceptual aliasing.
3D instance segmentation ▼
Identifying and separating individual object instances in 3D space — not just "there is a chair here" but "here is chair #1, here is chair #2, and here is the table." Harder than 2D because 3D data is sparse and objects often partially occlude each other. Takmaz's SceneFun3D (CVPR 2024, Oral) goes further: it also labels what each object affords, enabling task-relevant queries like "find a surface I can place this box on."
Localizability estimation ▼
Predicting when LiDAR-based localization will be reliable vs. unreliable — before committing to a position estimate. In a featureless corridor or open field, there aren't enough distinct scan features to pin down location precisely. Nubert's PhD contribution: giving robots a self-awareness about their own localization confidence, so they can be cautious when uncertain rather than confidently wrong. Safety-critical for real-world deployment.

// Plain-English Technical Glossary
🎮 Reinforcement Learning (RL)
A way to train AI by giving rewards for good behavior and penalties for bad behavior — like training an animal. The robot doesn't get told what to do; it figures it out through millions of trial-and-error attempts in simulation.
🌉 Sim-to-Real Transfer
Training a robot inside a virtual simulation (cheaper, faster, safe), then deploying that training on a real physical robot. The challenge: simulation is never a perfect copy of reality. Flexion's key skill is making this transfer reliable.
📡 LiDAR
Light Detection and Ranging. A sensor that fires laser pulses and measures how long they take to bounce back, creating a precise 3D map of the environment (called a "point cloud"). Like radar but with light and much higher resolution.
🗺️ SLAM
Simultaneous Localization and Mapping. The robot builds a map of its environment while simultaneously figuring out where it is within that map — at the same time. This is hard because you need the map to know where you are, but you need to know where you are to build the map.
🧮 Transformer Architecture
The neural network architecture behind GPT, BERT, and most modern AI. It processes all parts of an input simultaneously using "attention" — figuring out which parts matter most for a given output. Flexion uses transformers for both language understanding and whole-body robot control.
🔗 Vision-Language-Action (VLA) Model
An AI model that takes in visual data (camera images), language instructions, and outputs physical actions. It's the three-way bridge: see the world, understand the instruction, move the body. Key to Flexion's approach.
☁️ Point Cloud
A collection of millions of 3D data points (x, y, z coordinates) representing the surface of objects in space. LiDAR sensors produce point clouds. Julian Nubert and Ayça Takmaz both work with point cloud data to help robots perceive their environment.
📋 Policy (in RL)
The "brain" of a trained robot — the set of rules it has learned for deciding what action to take in any given situation. A locomotion policy controls how the robot walks. A manipulation policy controls how it grasps objects.
🤸 Whole-Body Control (WBC)
Coordinating all of a robot's joints — arms, legs, torso, hands — at the same time to achieve a task. Rather than controlling each limb separately, WBC treats the entire body as one system. Essential for humanoid robots doing real work.
🏭 Synthetic Data
Artificially generated training data created inside simulators rather than collected in the real world. Cheaper, scalable, controllable. Flexion's key advantage: generating huge amounts of synthetic data in simulation to train robust models without expensive real-world data collection.
🔓 Open-Vocabulary Detection
A robot or AI that can identify any object described in words — not just a fixed list it was trained on. Uses large vision-language models like CLIP. Ayça Takmaz's OpenMask3D enables this in 3D space.
🖐️ Affordance Understanding
Understanding what actions an object enables — a handle affords grasping, a surface affords placing. Critical for robot manipulation. Ayça Takmaz's SceneFun3D specifically tackled this problem: not just identifying objects, but what the robot can DO with them.
⚡ Massively Parallel RL
Running thousands of robot simulations simultaneously on a single GPU, each learning from its own trial-and-error. Nikita Rudin's key innovation: this compresses months of training into minutes. The robot effectively experiences thousands of lifetimes of practice simultaneously.
🏋️ Isaac Gym / Isaac Lab
NVIDIA's simulation platform for robot reinforcement learning training. Co-developed by David Hoeller at NVIDIA. Now the industry standard — most robotics companies worldwide use it. Flexion's training pipelines are built on top of this.
🎲 Domain Randomization
During simulation training, randomly varying conditions — floor friction, lighting, object weights, sensor noise — so the robot learns to cope with uncertainty. When transferred to reality, the real-world variation falls within what it has already seen (in randomized form). Makes sim-to-real transfer much more reliable.
💬 LLM (Large Language Model)
AI models trained on vast amounts of text, capable of understanding and generating language. In robotics, they're used to interpret task instructions ("sort these boxes by color") and turn them into structured plans the robot can execute. The "brain" that understands what you want.

▸ Evaluating candidates — signal vs. noise
What signals differentiate a strong robotics RL researcher from a weak one?
Strong signals: Published in Science Robotics, ICRA, CoRL, or NeurIPS. Code on GitHub with stars. "Sim-to-real" in their work — not just simulation. Experience with real hardware. First or second author (not buried in a 20-person list). Contributed to open-source tools (Isaac Lab, legged_gym, Open3D SLAM).

Weak signals: Only pure ML with no robotics application. No real hardware experience. Only industry roles without research output. Papers only in workshops, not main conferences.
What should a recruiter know before screening a robotics RL researcher?
Read their papers — at least the abstract and intro. Know the difference between quadruped work (4-legged, Flexion's research background) and humanoid work (2-legged, current target). Two questions that reveal depth fast:

"What was the biggest sim-to-real transfer failure you experienced, and how did you address it?"

"What training infrastructure did you use — how many GPUs, how many parallel envs?"

// Hiring Architecture — Building for Scale Without Losing Speed

Deep-tech teams grow differently. The first hires happen through founders' networks and shared conviction. The next fifty require something more deliberate — but not heavier. The goal is structure that serves speed: consistent decisions, low candidate drop-off, and a process engineers trust enough to actually participate in.

▸ Foundations — what to build before you need it
01 / Intake
Define "great" before opening a role
Never post a job until the hiring manager can answer: what does this person own in 6 months, and what does excellent look like at 12? In engineering-led orgs this step gets skipped — the result is 4 months of screening candidates against an implicit bar no one agreed on. A one-page intake doc (3–5 ranked criteria, must-haves vs. nice-to-haves) takes 30 minutes and saves weeks.
02 / Scorecard
Evaluation by criteria, not impression
Structured scorecards don't slow engineers down — they make engineers' time count. Each interviewer assesses 1–2 criteria only, with a 1–4 rating and a written evidence requirement. This eliminates "I just had a good feeling" as a hiring signal, reduces recency bias from the last candidate seen, and creates a defensible paper trail when a hire doesn't work out.
03 / Pipeline
CRM before ATS — relationships before reqs
In a community this small, every senior candidate will encounter Flexion 2–3 times before they're ready to move. Track every conversation: conference chats, paper compliments, referral intros. An ATS tracks applicants. A CRM builds the pipeline that feeds the ATS. Tools: Ashby (combines both), or Notion + Clay for early stage. Starting from zero at every search is the most expensive mistake in niche hiring.
04 / Compensation
Leveling framework before the first equity dispute
Startups skip leveling until someone finds out a colleague with less experience earns more. By then, trust is damaged and attrition risk is real. A simple 3-level framework (IC1/IC2/IC3 with salary bands and equity ranges) takes a day to build and prevents months of firefighting. Publish the bands internally — transparency is a retention tool in research-heavy teams who expect intellectual honesty in all things.
05 / Referrals
Formalize the network before it becomes informal bias
Founder networks produce excellent early hires — but left unmanaged, they create monocultures. Formalize referrals: ask for them systematically (not just when desperate), track source-of-hire by demographic, and set an explicit ceiling (e.g. no more than 40% of hires from any single source). Incentivize referrals with time off rather than cash — it attracts more thoughtful recommendations from researchers who don't want to stake their reputation on a bad refer.
06 / Metrics
Four numbers that tell you if hiring is working
Avoid metrics theater. Four numbers are enough at this stage: time-to-offer (should be ≤21 days from first contact), offer acceptance rate (below 80% = process or comp problem), pipeline-to-hire ratio by stage (where are candidates dropping?), and 90-day retention rate (are hires surviving onboarding?). Review monthly with founders. Anything more is overhead, not signal.
▸ Pitfalls — where engineering-led hiring breaks down
Critical
The founder bottleneck
Every offer needs founder sign-off. This works at 5 hires. At 15 it slows everything to a crawl, and candidates notice. Fix: define a delegation threshold — founders approve role-opens and final offers above a certain level; TA and hiring manager handle everything in between. This isn't about losing control, it's about founders spending their credibility on the decisions that actually need it.
Critical
"We'll know it when we see it"
The most expensive phrase in hiring. Without a defined bar, every interviewer uses a different one. The result: inconsistent decisions, months of churn, and hires made on vibes that don't survive 90 days. The fix isn't bureaucracy — it's a single shared document per role, agreed before interviews start. Five criteria. That's it.
Critical
Speed pressure lowering the bar
A role open for 4 months creates enormous pressure to hire the best available candidate rather than the right one. The team ends up carrying a weak hire for 18 months — far more expensive than the open role. Hold the bar. Communicate clearly to the business what "right" looks like and why a near-miss is worse than a delay. This requires TA having enough credibility with founders to push back.
High risk
Interview process designed by committee
Engineers, left to their own devices, will design 6-round interview processes with 4-hour coding challenges. They're optimizing for certainty. Candidates with competing offers will drop. The sweet spot: 3 stages, max 4 hours total, decision within 48h of final round. Top researchers are evaluating Flexion as much as Flexion is evaluating them — the process itself is a signal about how the company operates.
High risk
Conference dependency
Sourcing only at ICRA or CoRL is a lagging indicator — the best candidates already have competing offers by the time they're at the conference. Conferences are relationship confirmation events, not discovery events. The pipeline needs to be built in the 6 months before through paper outreach, GitHub engagement, and warm intros. Arrive at ICRA knowing people by name, not handing out business cards.
High risk
"Culture fit" without definition
In a vibes-driven org, "culture fit" becomes shorthand for "reminds me of us" — which means hiring a monoculture. Worse, it's legally exposed. Replace it with "culture add": what does this person bring that we don't have? Define the operating principles explicitly (e.g. "bias to action," "direct feedback," "intellectual honesty") and evaluate against those, not against personality similarity.
Watch
No onboarding = silent attrition
A researcher joins and is immediately handed a laptop and a Slack invite. Three months later they're disengaged. Onboarding is a retention tool, not an HR formality. A lightweight 30/60/90 plan — written by the hiring manager before the start date — sets expectations, creates early wins, and signals that the company actually thought about what this person should be doing.
Watch
The ghost pipeline
No system for tracking past candidates means rebuilding from zero every search. Someone who was a near-miss 18 months ago might be perfect now — but if there's no record of the conversation, they don't exist. A minimal CRM (even a Notion table with name, date, context, and next touchpoint) prevents this. The robotics community is small enough that every relationship has a 70% chance of being relevant again.
▸ Candidate experience — why it matters more here than anywhere

The robotics research community is a small, dense graph. Everyone knows everyone. A researcher who has a bad experience in your interview process will mention it at the next lab meeting, conference dinner, or group chat. Conversely, a great experience — even from a declined candidate — generates referrals and ambient credibility.

  • 01
    Single point of contact, start to finish
    One TA person owns the relationship — not a different recruiter at each stage. Candidates shouldn't have to re-explain their background. They should feel like someone is tracking their journey, not processing their application.
  • 02
    Communicate the timeline before being asked
    Silence after an interview is the single biggest driver of candidate drop-off. Tell them: "You'll hear from us by Thursday." Then honor it. If the decision slips, proactively communicate. Researchers are used to clear deadlines and direct communication — treat them accordingly.
  • 03
    Feedback to declined candidates (where possible)
    Most companies don't do this. Which is exactly why doing it creates outsized goodwill. Even one sentence of honest feedback — "We went with someone with more real-hardware deployment experience" — turns a rejection into a positive memory. That candidate will still recommend Flexion to colleagues.
  • 04
    Respect for technical time
    Don't ask PhD researchers to do 4-hour take-home assignments as a first filter. It signals that their time isn't valued. Use async video intros, paper-based conversations, or a short 45-minute technical screen with a senior engineer instead. The best candidates have options — the process is already a filter for how you operate.
  • 05
    Offer experience = first day of onboarding
    The offer call is not a formality. It's the moment a candidate decides whether they trust the company. Come prepared: know the person's motivations, have a clear equity explanation ready, be able to answer "who will I be working with?" and "what does the first 90 days look like?" Close the offer on the call, not over three weeks of email.
